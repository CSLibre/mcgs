AI Coverage — Deep Dive
🤖 Digital Literacy — AI is central (extensive)
7TC.07 / 8TC.08 / 9TC.10
Prompt engineering & prompting frameworks (RTF, CORE) — explored across all 3 stages with increasing sophistication
7SW.04 / 8SW.02
AI use contributes to digital footprint; personal data stored and used to train AI systems
7SW.06 / 8SW.06 / 9SW.06
AI as barrier to sustained/deep learning — explored each stage with increasing responsibility for self-regulation
7DW.01
Predictions about Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) and Artificial Super Intelligence (ASI)
7DW.11 / 9DW.10
Affective AI — inputs, outputs, emotional recognition, applications and ethical risks
8DW.04
AI Slop — overproduction of AI-generated content contributing to information overload
8DW.09 / 9DW.09
Agentic AI — introduced in Stage 8, ethics and human responsibility explored in Stage 9
9SW.09
Anthropomorphic relationships with AI — emotional, social and security risks
7DW.03 / 8DW.03 / 9DW.03
AI algorithms influencing human decision-making — progresses from basic influence to data bias and echo chambers
💻 Computing — AI is contextual (introductory)
7CS.07
What AI is — computers taking information from surroundings to produce outputs based on processing
7CS.09
Applications of AI — image recognition and computer games as example contexts
8CS.08
Machine learning defined — systems that automatically learn and alter behaviour from experience
8CS.09
Augmented reality — identification in familiar contexts including education and entertainment
8CS.10
Autonomous programming and AI in robotics — how robots are programmed using AI
9CS.10
Machine learning scenarios — a range of real-world contexts where ML is applied
9CS.11
Industry 4.0 — benefits and risks of computerisation of traditional manufacturing (technical angle)
Key difference: Computing explains the technical mechanism of AI/ML. Digital Literacy explores the human, ethical and societal implications of using AI.

Teaching Approach & Delivery
📱
Digital Literacy
Can be delivered as standalone subject, integrated across other subjects, or outside formal curriculum
No specific software required — open access tools and non-subscription AI platforms are sufficient
Discussion, scenario-based learning, and critical reflection are central to many objectives
No Checkpoint test — teacher assessment based on ongoing learner progress and discussion
~30 hours per stage (approximately 1 hour per week)
Strong links to Wellbeing, English, Science, Art, Global Perspectives and all other subjects
Version 3.0 (Feb 2026) — substantially updated to reflect generative AI availability
💻
Computing
Primarily delivered as a standalone subject with dedicated teaching time
Recommends Python (IDLE or online-python.com) and micro:bit for physical computing
Microsoft Excel and Access recommended for data strand; pen-and-paper "unplugged" activities encouraged
No Checkpoint test — classroom and CEM Baseline Test assessments
~60 hours per stage (approximately 2 hours per week)
Cross-curricular links to Science, Mathematics and Digital Literacy
Version 2.0 (Apr 2022) — stable curriculum; does not yet deeply address generative AI
